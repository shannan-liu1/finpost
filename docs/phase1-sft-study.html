1. Experiment setup

This was a supervised fine-tuning ablation on Qwen 2.5-0.5B, a 0.5-billion-parameter language model trained from scratch on math reasoning data. The goal was to understand which data mixture — single-domain or combined — produces the best generalization, and how many training steps are actually needed before overfitting sets in.

Three training arms, each run to 2000 steps with checkpoints saved every 500 steps:

Training datasets

  • GSM8K — ~7,500 elementary school word problems with step-by-step solutions. Short reasoning chains, formulaic structure, answers are small integers.
  • MATH — ~7,500 competition-level problems (algebra, number theory, calculus). Long and varied reasoning chains, answers in \boxed{} format.
  • gsm8k_only arm: trains only on GSM8K — 2000 steps × 16 = 32k draws from 7.5k examples (~4 passes through the data).
  • math_only arm: trains only on MATH — same compute, ~4 passes through MATH.
  • combined arm: trains on GSM8K + MATH interleaved — same compute, ~2 passes through each dataset. Half the per-dataset exposure, but covers both.

Loss function

Standard masked cross-entropy. Only response tokens contribute to the loss — prompt tokens are masked with IGNORE_INDEX. All response tokens are weighted equally. There is no outcome-based signal: the model is trained to reproduce the full reasoning chain, not just to get the final answer right.

2. Full results

Accuracy = fraction of 500 examples where the extracted final answer matched the ground truth exactly. Parse success rate = fraction where the model produced output the answer extractor could read at all. A model can fail in two ways: produce a wrong answer (parse succeeds, accuracy fails) or produce unreadable output (parse fails, accuracy definitely fails).

3. Key findings

Finding 1 — Combined wins on breadth

combined-500 achieves the highest GSM8K accuracy of any checkpoint: 0.276. This beats gsm8k_only's best (0.250 at step 1000) even though combined only saw roughly half as many GSM8K examples by step 500. Exposure to both domains during training acts as regularization — the model cannot overfit a single format, so it learns more general reasoning patterns.

Finding 2 — Step 500 is the sweet spot; 2000 steps overfit

Every arm peaks at or before step 1000, then degrades. The combined arm peaks at step 500 (its very first checkpoint), the gsm8k_only arm peaks at step 1000, and the math_only arm peaks at step 500 on MATH before declining all the way to 0.160 by step 2000. Approximately 75% of the compute budget in this run was spent in the overfit zone. For a future SFT run on a 0.5B model with this dataset size (~7.5k examples per source), 500–750 steps is likely the right budget.

Finding 3 — math_only catastrophically forgets GSM8K format

After training exclusively on MATH, the model produces nearly unreadable output when given GSM8K prompts: parse success rate collapses to 0.082 at step 500 and never recovers past 0.106. This is not a matter of getting wrong answers — the model stops producing parseable answers at all. It has learned MATH's \boxed{} output convention so thoroughly that it cannot produce the plain #### format GSM8K expects. This is format forgetting, not reasoning forgetting.

Finding 4 — math_only's MATH accuracy declines while parse rate climbs

The most revealing signal in the data: math_only's MATH parse success rate increases from 0.784 to 0.838 as training continues, while accuracy falls from 0.206 to 0.160. The model is learning to produce correctly-formatted \boxed{answer} output more reliably — but filling in wrong values. It has memorized the answer wrapper, not the reasoning that should go inside it. More training = better format, worse math.

Finding 5 — math_only wins narrowly on MATH, but only at step 500

The best MATH accuracy is math_only-500 (0.206), which edges out combined-500 (0.186) and gsm8k_only-1000 (0.198). But this lead exists only at step 500 — by step 2000, math_only has degraded to 0.160, worse than every other arm. The specialist win is real but fragile and does not survive continued training.

Finding 6 — combined-500 is the best single checkpoint

If you must choose one checkpoint to carry into the next phase: combined-500. It achieves the highest GSM8K accuracy (0.276), remains competitive on MATH (0.186), and — unlike math_only — does not catastrophically forget how to answer GSM8K questions. It is the only checkpoint that is genuinely strong on both benchmarks simultaneously.

4. Concepts

What is SFT doing?

Concept

Supervised fine-tuning trains the model to predict the next token given all previous tokens, with the loss computed only on the response side (not the prompt). The training signal says: "given this question and the start of this reasoning chain, the next token should be X." It is imitation learning — the model learns to reproduce the style and structure of the training examples, not to verify whether its reasoning is correct.

This is why SFT is fast and stable but has a ceiling. The model never gets explicit feedback that its reasoning was wrong; it only gets rewarded for predicting the training tokens. If a training example has correct reasoning, the model can learn that pattern. If it finds a shortcut that produces plausible-looking text, SFT will not penalize it.

Why does SFT overfit at 2000 steps here?

Concept

With ~7,500 training examples and batch size 16, one pass through the data takes ~470 steps. By step 2000 the model has seen each example roughly 4 times. A 0.5B-parameter model has a lot of capacity relative to a 7,500-example dataset — it can begin memorizing specific solution formats rather than learning general reasoning. The overfitting shows up as declining accuracy (the model gets less right) even as training loss continues to fall (the model is getting better at reproducing the training text verbatim).

The fix is early stopping — in this experiment, step 500 was sufficient. Validation loss monitoring (watching eval loss on held-out examples during training) would have caught this in real time.

Catastrophic forgetting

Concept

When a model fine-tunes strongly on a new distribution, it can overwrite the weights that encoded its previous knowledge. This is called catastrophic forgetting. In this experiment, math_only training on MATH's \boxed{} format overwrites the model's ability to produce GSM8K's #### format. The model has not forgotten how to do arithmetic — it has forgotten the output convention that the GSM8K parser expects.

Combined training mitigates forgetting by forcing the model to maintain both formats simultaneously throughout training. It never gets far enough from either distribution to forget the other.

Outcome evaluation vs process evaluation

Concept

This study measures outcome: did the model produce the correct final answer? It says nothing about whether the reasoning chain that produced the answer was valid. A model could write completely wrong reasoning and arrive at the right answer by coincidence, or write perfect reasoning and fail at the final extraction step.

The decomposition into parse success rate and accuracy gives a partial view: parse success tells you whether the model is producing readable output at all; accuracy tells you whether the readable output is correct. But whether the reasoning in between is valid — that requires reading individual examples from the details_*.csv files, which the eval produces per checkpoint.

This is the core motivation for outcome-based training methods (DPO, GRPO): they only reinforce reasoning chains that actually produced correct answers, so the model learns "this style of reasoning works" rather than "this is what training-set solutions look like."

Why did combined outperform the GSM8K specialist on GSM8K?

Concept

This is the most counter-intuitive result and the most important one. Combined saw half as many GSM8K examples per step, yet beat the specialist. There are two likely mechanisms:

Regularization effect: alternating between two different problem types prevents the model from overfitting the GSM8K format. The MATH examples act as a regularizer — every MATH step nudges the model slightly away from pure GSM8K format memorization, so it has to learn something more general.

Transfer of reasoning structure: MATH problems are harder and have more varied solution structures. Training on them may build general mathematical reasoning capabilities that transfer back to GSM8K. The combined model may be learning "how to reason about numbers" while the specialist is learning "what GSM8K solutions look like."

5. Implications for DPO

These SFT results directly motivate the next phase and inform the setup decisions.

Starting checkpoint: combined-500

DPO starts from a frozen SFT checkpoint (the reference model) and trains a policy model to prefer correct-answer completions over incorrect ones. The starting checkpoint should be one that already produces correct answers at a non-trivial rate — otherwise there is nothing for DPO to reinforce. Combined-500 (0.276 GSM8K, 0.186 MATH) is the best starting point: it has real signal on both benchmarks and has not yet overfit its output format.

What DPO should fix

SFT's ceiling is set by format imitation — it cannot distinguish good reasoning from plausible-looking reasoning. DPO's preference pairs are constructed by sampling multiple completions from combined-500, grading them with the exact-answer verifier, and building chosen/rejected pairs. The training signal then explicitly says: "when given this prompt, the chosen response is better than the rejected one."

The hypothesis: DPO will push accuracy above the SFT ceiling by reinforcing the specific reasoning patterns that happen to produce correct answers, and suppressing the patterns that produce wrong answers even when they look superficially reasonable.

The ceiling question

MATH accuracy has been stubbornly flat across all arms: 0.186–0.206 is the range. Even the best checkpoint (math_only-500) barely exceeds 0.206. DPO will test whether outcome-based training from combined-500 can push meaningfully past this ceiling, or whether 0.2 is approximately the limit of what a 0.5B model can achieve on competition-level MATH problems with this training approach. If DPO also plateaus near 0.2 on MATH, the bottleneck is likely model capacity, not training method — and the right answer is a larger model, not a better algorithm.

Summary of decisions this study locks in

  • DPO starting checkpoint: combined-500 — validated empirically as the best SFT checkpoint.
  • Future SFT runs: 500 steps, not 2000. The extra 1500 steps were wasted compute.
  • Data mixture: combined training is the right default for a model expected to generalize across benchmarks.
  • Eval baseline: the SFT result (combined-500) is the bar DPO must beat to be declared a net improvement.
